## Abstract

The experimental observation of Floquet states in submicrometric magnetic vortices under weak magnonic excitation represents a conceptual shift in nonequilibrium physics. Rather than relying on highenergy laser forcing, these results reveal that coherent, lowamplitude excitation is sufficient to induce stable, discrete dynamical regimes. This whitepaper interprets these findings as manifestations of toroidal electromagnetic systems capable of adaptive reconfiguration when symmetry is lost.

By integrating Floquet dynamics with the Theory of Learning by Exception (TAE) and the METFI framework, we propose that such systems do not merely respond to stimuli but *select* new operational states when confronted with resonant exceptions. This logic is shown to be formally invariant across scales, from nanomagnetic vortices to biological and planetary systems.

## 1. Introduction

Traditional approaches to nonequilibrium control assume that new dynamical regimes require increased energetic input. Recent results from the HelmholtzZentrum DresdenRossendorf challenge this assumption by demonstrating that weak, coherent excitation suffices when structural resonance is achieved.

This observation forces a reconsideration of how complex systems transition between stable regimes, suggesting that topology and phase coherence dominate over raw energy.

## 2. Floquet Theory Beyond Linear Periodicity

Floquet theory classically addresses linear systems with timeperiodic Hamiltonians. However, real magnetic vortices exhibit internal topology, memory, and nonlinearity. The observed Floquet states therefore represent *emergent discrete attractors* rather than trivial periodic solutions.

> [!NOTE]
> In such systems, time effectively becomes discretized, functioning as an operational lattice.

---

## 3. Magnetic Vortices as Toroidal Systems

Magnetic vortices are characterized by closed circulation of magnetization around a core, forming a toroidal electromagnetic architecture. This geometry supports internal modes that can store and redistribute energy coherently.

Loss of perfect symmetry does not destabilize the system; instead, it opens channels for discrete reorganization.

---

## 4. Weak Magnonic Excitation and Structural Resonance

The Dresden experiments demonstrate that magnonic waves tuned to internal frequencies trigger Floquet states without destructive forcing.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> The system responds not to amplitude, but to *coherence*.

This principle mirrors resonance phenomena across biological and geophysical systems.

## 5. Learning by Exception (TAE): Physical Formalization

TAE posits that learning occurs when a system encounters a perturbation incompatible with its existing operational regime. In physical terms:

* Periodic excitation defines a stimulus
* Structural resonance defines the exception
* A Floquet state defines the learned configuration

This transforms learning from a cognitive metaphor into a physical principle.

## 6. Temporal Discretization and Dynamic Memory

Floquet states encode memory dynamically rather than statically. Stability emerges from repetition, phase locking, and coherence over cycles.

This mechanism parallels neural oscillations, cardiac rhythms, and endocrine cycles.
